What's in this document.

This document specifies the visual identity for Standard House Partners, including the wordmark system, colophon, color palette, typography, and applied mockups across the firm's primary surfaces. I · Wordmark

The horizontal lockup.

The Standard House Partners wordmark is a single line. "Standard House" sits in Source Serif 4 at 400 weight in ink black. "Partners" sits immediately adjacent in the same family at the same weight and size, set in editorial red. No divider, no rule, no italic. The color shift alone carries the typographic gesture — the firm's classification gets the chromatic accent while its name holds the structural ink. The lockup reads as one composed phrase: a name that includes its own classification.

Primary lockup · Display size

Display size: used at 48 px and above. Optical letter-spacing tightened to −0.4 to match the editorial-publication register. The space between "House" and "Partners" is a single regular word-space — no extra padding, no kerning intervention.

Reversed · Dark applications

In reverse, "Standard House" shifts to ivory and "Partners" shifts to lightened red (#C95252) to preserve the chromatic gesture against dark backgrounds while maintaining accessible contrast. Never use the primary red (#8B2E2E) on ink — it loses too much contrast and reads as a muddy near-monochrome.

Usage rules
·Never set the wordmark below 18 px. Below this size, the chromatic gesture on "Partners" loses legibility.
·Never italicize "Partners." The italic was an earlier direction; the locked treatment is the same font, same weight, same size, color shift alone.
·Never separate the elements with a divider, em-dash, rule, or extra spacing. The lockup is one phrase.
·Never stack the wordmark vertically. The stacked masthead direction was retired during refinement.
·Clear space: minimum padding equal to the cap-height of "S" on all four sides.
II · Colophon

The § as primary mark.

The section mark is the firm's distinguishing visual signature. Set in Source Serif 4 in ink black, the § operates as a printer's colophon — the historical mark that signed a publisher's work product. The firm uses the § structurally inside report pages (§ 4.1, § 4.2) for dimension citations, so the symbol is consistent with the firm's existing visual vocabulary rather than imposed on it. "Standard House" sits beneath the § in editorial red; the small-caps sans label "PARTNERS" sits below in the firm's classification register.

Colophon · Full form

Three typographic registers, each doing different work: the § as the brand's distinguishing symbol; "Standard House" as the firm's name carrying the chromatic accent; "PARTNERS" as the classification in small-caps sans — the same register as a publication's masthead credit line.

At compact size, "PARTNERS" drops off — the colophon retains its full identity through § and "Standard House." At favicon size, only the § survives, which is sufficient: the § alone is recognizable to any returning visitor as the firm's mark.

Reversed · Dark applications

Reversed treatment: § and PARTNERS shift to ivory, "Standard House" shifts to lightened red to preserve chromatic contrast. The brand's color logic remains intact in dark contexts.

III · Color Palette

Ink, red, ivory.

The palette is ink black on newsprint ivory, with a single editorial red accent. Color in this brand is not decoration; it is a structural element of the page — a rule, a drop cap, the chromatic gesture on "Partners," the kicker above a report headline. Most pages will use none of the accent red at all.

IV · Typography

Three roles, one serif, one sans.

Source Serif 4 carries the brand. Designed by Frank Grießhammer for long-form editorial reading, it operates across display, body, and quotation registers without changing family. Inter supports it for working contexts — mastheads, captions, navigation, data tables. Both are open-licensed: no commercial licensing costs, no font-loading constraints, no risk of license expiry. The brand is engineered for the work it asks of itself.

Accent Inter, 11/14 Mastheads, captions, data tables, navigation, metadata
Engagement memorandum — Section IV PREPARED BY STANDARD HOUSE PARTNERS  ·  CONFIDENTIAL  ·  VOL. III  ·  THE FOLLOWING ANALYSIS IS INTENDED SOLELY FOR THE DEAL TEAM IDENTIFIED ON THE COVER. NOT FOR DISTRIBUTION.
Typographic principles
·Serif body, sans support. All long-form reading is in Source Serif. Inter appears only in caption, masthead, and data contexts — never in the body of an argument.
·Drop caps signal section opens. The drop cap appears once per major section, set in editorial red. Never decorative; always at the lead of substantive analysis.
·Italic carries emphasis, not chrome. Italics are used for genuine emphasis or for the firm's classification labels (e.g. "Partner · Founder"). Never for decoration.
·Small caps signal classification. "PARTNERS," section labels in mastheads, and rating tags are all set in Inter small-caps at 0.22–0.28em letter-spacing.
·Mono for numbers and codes. IBM Plex Mono appears in volume numbers, report citations, dates, and any context where alignment of figures matters.
V · Applied — Report Cover

The Diligence Report.

The Diligence Report is the firm's primary deliverable and the brand's most consequential public artifact. Its cover sets the typographic and chromatic logic that the rest of the report inherits: masthead-style banner, kicker in red, headline in serif at display size, italic subtitle, thin red rule, classification metadata, and a closing footer that carries both the firm's name and its tagline.

VI · Applied — Stationery

Business card.

The business card carries the colophon as a corner detail and the partial wordmark ("Standard House" + small-caps PARTNERS) as the firm's classification block. The personal name takes the dominant typographic position — the firm operates behind the work; the principal in front of it. Two treatments: newsprint ivory for client-facing applications, ink for formal occasions and senior partner cards.

VII · Applied — Website

Homepage hero.

The website carries the same editorial logic as the report. Newsprint ivory background, a hard masthead rule under the navigation, serif headline at 56 px, italic subtitle, two CTAs — primary in ink, secondary as an underlined link. The horizontal wordmark sits in the navigation at 22 px so "Partners" reads cleanly in red against the navigation bar.

VIII · Applied — Email Signature

Correspondence.

The email signature is the digital equivalent of the business card. Serif name, italic title, a single red rule, the wordmark in horizontal lockup form, then contact in sans, then the tagline in italic serif as a soft closing line. HTML signatures should be tested across Gmail, Outlook, and Apple Mail to ensure font fallbacks behave; web-safe Georgia is the acceptable substitute when Source Serif 4 is unavailable.

The signature is approximately 220 px wide rendered — the same width as a typical email body column. The horizontal lockup at 16 px is the smallest size the wordmark should appear; below this, the chromatic gesture on "Partners" can degrade in some email clients. Test before locking the signature for the firm.

IX · Applied — The Standard

Newsletter masthead.

The Standard is the firm's monthly newsletter. The name doubles as a promise: each issue applies the firm's standard to a single recent deal, channel risk, or market development. The masthead carries the full editorial-publication treatment — the firm's name and issue metadata above a hard rule, the title set at 88 px in display serif, an italic deck below, and a double rule before the lead article. This is the brand at its most ceremonial register.

From Standard House Partners · Issue No. 003
MARCH MMXXVI

The Standard

A monthly review of marketing risk in middle-market M&A.
"When a single keyword cluster carries half a target company's traffic, the diligence question is not whether the risk exists. The question is whether the buyer has priced it."

The newsletter masthead inverts the wordmark's color logic: in The Standard, the word "Standard" itself carries the red and the italic — reading the title as both the name of the publication and a chromatic reinforcement of the brand's central premise. The deck below introduces the lead article in a single quoted sentence, set in italic display serif.

X · Summary

What this adds up to.

A firm that operates as if it is the reference. Editorial, typographic, restrained. Built around a single discipline. The visual world reinforces the institutional voice: every element does work, nothing is decorative, and the firm appears most credible when it performs least.
Visual Direction · V1 · Internal
Brand Principles
·Restraint signals confidence. The firms that need to over-design are the firms without institutional weight. Standard House Partners' substance comes from operating as if it doesn't need to perform anything.
·The lockup is one phrase. "Standard House" and "Partners" sit adjacent, no divider, no extra spacing, no italic. The color shift on "Partners" carries the entire typographic gesture.
·The § is structural, not decorative. Every appearance of the section mark does work — as the colophon, as a citation mark inside reports (§ 4.1, § 4.2), as the closing mark of formal documents. It is the brand's punctuation.
·Serif typography carries the institutional voice. No sans-serif appears in display or body contexts. The brand reads the way an editorial publication reads — with discipline, hierarchy, and restraint.
·One accent, used sparingly. Editorial red appears at most once per page. It is structural, never decorative. The brand's chromatic discipline is its primary visible commitment.
·The firm operates behind the work. No founders pictured. No personalities promoted. The work product is the brand's voice.
